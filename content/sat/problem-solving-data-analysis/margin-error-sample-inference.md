# Margin of Error and Sample Inference — SAT Math Guide

Margin of error and sample inference SAT questions test your ability to interpret statistical data and make predictions about populations. These problems involve understanding confidence intervals, sample statistics, and how to draw conclusions from survey data. You'll see 2-3 questions like this in the Problem Solving and Data Analysis domain. Master these concepts and you'll confidently tackle any statistics question the Digital SAT throws at you.

## What You Need to Know

• **Margin of error** — the range of uncertainty around a sample statistic
• **Sample statistic** — a value calculated from sample data (like sample mean or proportion)
• **Population parameter** — the true value for the entire population
• **Confidence interval** — sample statistic ± margin of error
• **Sample size effect** — larger samples generally have smaller margins of error
• **Representative samples** — samples that accurately reflect the population

> 📐 KEY FORMULA: Confidence Interval = Sample Statistic ± Margin of Error
> 💡 PRO TIP: The margin of error tells you how far off your sample might be from the true population value.

## How to Solve Margin of Error and Sample Inference on the SAT

### Example Question 1 — Medium Difficulty

A polling company surveyed 400 randomly selected voters and found that 240 support a ballot measure. The margin of error for this survey is ±3.5%. Which of the following best represents the range of the percentage of all voters who support the ballot measure?

A) 56.5% to 63.5%
B) 57% to 63%
C) 60% to 60%
D) 236.5 to 243.5

**Solution:**
Step 1: Calculate the sample proportion: 240/400 = 0.60 = 60%
Step 2: Apply the margin of error: 60% ± 3.5%
Step 3: Find the range: 60% - 3.5% = 56.5% and 60% + 3.5% = 63.5%

✅ Answer: A — The confidence interval extends 3.5 percentage points in each direction from the sample proportion.

### Example Question 2 — Hard Difficulty

A researcher wants to estimate the average height of students at a university. She randomly samples 100 students and finds a sample mean of 68.2 inches with a margin of error of ±1.8 inches at a 95% confidence level. If she increases her sample size to 400 students, which statement about the new margin of error is most likely true?

A) It will be exactly ±0.9 inches
B) It will be smaller than ±1.8 inches
C) It will be larger than ±1.8 inches
D) It will remain ±1.8 inches

**Solution:**
Step 1: Understand that sample size affects margin of error inversely
Step 2: Recognize that quadrupling the sample size (100 to 400) typically halves the margin of error
Step 3: Apply the relationship: larger samples produce more precise estimates with smaller margins of error

✅ Answer: B — Increasing sample size decreases the margin of error, making estimates more precise.

## Common SAT Math Mistakes to Avoid

❌ Mistake: Confusing the sample statistic with the population parameter
✅ Fix: Remember that samples estimate populations — the true population value falls within the confidence interval

❌ Mistake: Adding/subtracting margin of error incorrectly
✅ Fix: Always go both directions: sample statistic ± margin of error gives you the full range

❌ Mistake: Thinking larger samples increase margin of error
✅ Fix: Larger samples decrease margin of error and increase precision

❌ Mistake: Misinterpreting what the confidence interval represents
✅ Fix: The interval estimates where the true population parameter likely falls, not where individual data points fall

## Practice Question — Try It Yourself

A survey of 500 high school students found that 35% plan to major in STEM fields, with a margin of error of ±4%. A second survey of 200 students from the same population found that 32% plan to major in STEM fields. Which statement is most reasonable?

A) The second survey contradicts the first survey
B) The second survey confirms the first survey is wrong
C) The results are consistent given the margin of error
D) More data is needed to draw any conclusions

## Key Takeaways for the SAT

• Confidence intervals show the range where the true population parameter likely falls
• Margin of error creates uncertainty around sample statistics — larger samples reduce this uncertainty
• When comparing surveys, check if results fall within each other's confidence intervals
• SAT math questions often ask you to interpret what margin of error means in context
• Remember that individual data points don't need to fall within the confidence interval — only the population parameter does
